I can usually tell from the first lesson which student will do well in a second language. There's one sign I look for. It isn't intelligence. It isn't confidence. It isn't perfect behaviour. It's something else entirely — and I saw it in Pan from day one.

It was the opening day of my language school, thirteen years ago now. I was running an information session for the parents of prospective students, and the children had come along too. Some were on devices. A few were crawling around the floor. Most were gazing out the window. But one five-year-old, Pan, had a laser focus on everything in the room — she tracked us as we moved, watched the slides, listened to every word. Her parents enrolled her that day, and from the first lesson on she brought that same focus through the door with her.

At the time I didn't have the words for what I was seeing. I do now. Before a child can remember, speak, read or practise a language, they first have to *notice* it. And noticing begins with attention.

## The other end of the room

There's always a student at the other end of the classroom. The one whose focus moves like a lighthouse beam — sweeping across the room, rarely stopping long enough to land. The worksheet. The window. The pencil case. The friend beside them. The sound outside. Attention everywhere but you.

It's tempting to read this as a deficit — Pan *has* attention, this child *lacks* it — and to fix it by making the lesson more fun. But that gets attention wrong, and the correction comes from an unlikely place. In *On Looking*, Alexandra Horowitz describes attention not as a quantity you have more or less of, but as a kind of judgement:

> Attention is an intentional, unapologetic discriminator. It asks what is relevant right now, and gears us up to notice only that.

Read that again with the distracted child in mind. Their attention isn't broken. It's working perfectly — it has simply decided the pencil case is the most relevant thing in the room. Pan didn't have *more* attention than the others. She had made a different decision: that everything happening here was relevant, and she was geared up to notice it. Both children are discriminating. The only difference is what won.

## The locked gate

This matters more than it first appears, because of *where* attention sits in the act of learning. The cognitive scientist Daniel Willingham draws a simple map of the mind: the world around us, a working memory where thinking happens, and a long-term memory where it's stored. Attention is the gate between the first two.

Not one stage among many. The gate everything else has to pass through. If you don't have a child's attention, that gate is locked — and your explanation, your activity, your beautiful worksheet, your hours of planning never make it to the other side. Scott Young, who writes about how people learn, puts the consequence plainly:

> Most of the time, we don't remember something easily because we weren't paying enough attention to it when we first encountered it.

We tend to blame forgetting on a weak memory. Often the memory never formed, because the moment never made it through the gate.

## A better question

So the teacher's job isn't really to "make the lesson fun." Fun is just one more thing competing for the discriminator's vote, and it doesn't always win. The job is quieter and harder: to make the lesson *read as relevant*, so the child's own attention turns towards it and the gate swings open.

That can be small. One move I use is a designed half-second between the instruction and the action — a deliberate pause that gives attention time to land before the doing begins. Nothing clever; it just stops the gate slamming shut on the next thing before the last one got through.

When I look at those two students now, I'm not seeing a naturally attentive child and a distracted one. I'm seeing the gate. And I'm asking a better question than "why won't they pay attention?"

How do I help open it?
